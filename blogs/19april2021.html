Dining table

“Baba, I was thinking, if we could open a 4-star hotel where only momos are sold, wouldn’t it be cool? If we do proper marketing, advertisements, and celebrity endorsements, we can make a fortune out of it!” I presented my business idea on the dinner table.


“Uhmm,” he replied.


“We can create a brand, you know like that of the Himalayan Java, Pizza Hut or Nanglo, which specializes in one category and just doubles down on it. After a few years, we can open another hotel of the same brand in Pokhara, then Chitwan, then Biratnagar, and so on. Within a few years, woohoo, we’re freaking millionaires!” I couldn’t control my excitement.


We discussed the initial capital, the hiring process, the best location for the hotel, and even the brand name.

My brother intervened, “It’s not that easy as it sounds. And also there are other problems like quality control, standard food taste, and so on.”
“Yea. And that ‘few years’ that you’re talking about can easily turn into a decade or so,” mom added.


Conversations like these frequently sprout out on the dinner table of my house. The kitchen turns into a meeting hall: my dad’s a board member, I’m the CEO, and my mom and brother are partners. Roles change depending on the conversation but everyone gets to present their views.


The other day, I was explaining why we should all take vitamin and mineral supplements. A few weeks ago, we were talking about how quickly Dubai turned into a luxury-city. I remember, recently my dad was talking about how much potential a sausage factory has in Nepal. I later researched a bit and found that it would take 3.5cr just to buy a sausage-making-machine. (The idea didn’t move forward.)


I love this!


Not talking about the business ideas. But the conversations. During this period of uncertainty and pandemic, I’m grateful for the fact that I get to spend quality time with my family and have fun conversations with them.


Again, the quote from the Avengers: Endgame─”No amount of money ever bought a second of time.”


I know for sure when I’ll be 50-something, I’ll look back on my life and miss these moments with my mom and dad. So why not live these moments fully while I can?
